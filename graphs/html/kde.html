Demystifying KDE

Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) is a popular tool in Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA). Think of it as a smooth, continuous version of a histogram.

1 The Data Points

Each observation in your data is represented as a mark on the bottom line (the blue ticks).

👉 Try it: Click anywhere inside the white graph to add new data points!

2 The "Kernel" (The Bumps)

Instead of putting data into rigid bins like a histogram, KDE places a smooth "bump" (a curve) exactly over every single data point.

3 The Bandwidth (Smoothness)

Bandwidth dictates how wide the bumps are.
• Too narrow: The graph becomes noisy and spiky (overfitting).
• Too wide: The graph becomes too flat and hides details (underfitting).

Spiky Bandwidth: 8 Flat

4 The Final KDE Curve

The final thick dark curve is literally just all the individual colorful bumps added together! Where bumps overlap, the final curve gets taller, indicating a high density of data.
